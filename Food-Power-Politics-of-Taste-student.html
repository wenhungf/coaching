Food, Power and the Politics of Taste

§Discussion article

When we sit down to eat, we rarely think we are doing anything political. Yet three very different books — a prize-winning Taiwanese novel, a famous American travel book from 1880, and a recent thesis about military-occupied Okinawa — all make the same quiet argument: food is never just food. What we cook, what we praise, who serves whom, and whose recipe gets called “authentic” are all questions of power. This reading puts the three side by side.

Presented as the rediscovered 1954 memoir of a Japanese novelist, Aoyama Chizuko, Taiwan Travelogue is in fact a 2020 novel by the Taiwanese writer Yáng Shuāng-zǐ, brilliantly translated into English by Lin King. It is a metafiction: a pseudo-translation wrapped in a fictional apparatus of prefaces, footnotes and an afterword. On the surface it is a warm, mouth-watering travelogue about two women touring 1930s Taiwan — then a colony of Japan — and eating their way through twelve local dishes.

But the framing is the point. The narrator, Chizuko, has an enormous appetite and an even bigger curiosity. She tells herself she wants to know the “real” Taiwan, not the official version served at government banquets. She praises every dish she meets. And far from being innocent, that praise is exactly what the novel asks us to examine.

Consider how Chizuko eats. She breaks each dish down into colors, textures and aromas; she compares humble street food to refined Japanese cooking; she turns a bowl of braised pork over rice into a small work of art. Her admiration is real. Yet what she is really doing is measuring Taiwan against a Japanese standard and then generously giving it her approval. This is the colonial gaze in action: the powerful visitor who looks, tastes, and judges, all while believing she is being open-minded. Her warmth is genuine and condescending at the same time — a double-edged sword.

Beside her walks her Taiwanese interpreter, Wáng Chiēn-hò, whom Chizuko fondly (and a little patronizingly) nicknames Chi-chan. Chi-chan introduces the food but rarely explains what it means to her. Her silence is not emptiness; it is the most eloquent thing in the book. She knows the visitor cannot fully understand what she is eating, and she refuses to perform her own culture on demand. Through her, the novel quietly exposes the asymmetry beneath the friendship: one woman is free to savor and leave; the other lives with the consequences. The food they share is not so much a bridge as a mirror.

Having survived the war and the end of empire, Chi-chan is the one who, decades later, is said to have translated Chizuko's book — so the the colonized interpreter ends up holding the last word. In a story about who gets to speak, that detail gives voice to the very person the original travelogue had turned into a charming sidekick.

Now travel back to 1880 and across the world. In A Tramp Abroad, Mark Twain tours Europe and grumbles, gloriously, about the food. European coffee is weak; the steak is like leather; the bread has no character. Then, in a famous chapter, he writes out a long inventory of the American dishes he is homesick for — fried chicken, Virginia ham, buckwheat cakes with maple syrup, corn on the cob.

Twain's tone is the opposite of Chizuko's. Where she is gentle and admiring, he is loud, irreverent and full of hyperbole. He uses mockery to tear down European pretension, and then he builds American identity back up through food. In contrast to the polished European table, his rugged, generous home cooking becomes proof of national character. His longing for a real American breakfast is really an assertion of pride — the defiance of a young, industrializing nation that refuses to bow to old Europe.

So both books use food to draw a border between “us” and “them.” But the direction of power runs the opposite way. Chizuko looks down on a colony with affection; Twain looks up at an older culture and sticks out his tongue. Whereas Chizuko's praise quietly conceals control, Twain's complaints openly mock it. Reading them together reveals the same tool — the travelogue — being used for two different kinds of power dynamics.

The third text turns the theme from fiction to history. A 2022 thesis from the University of the Ryukyus traces how American food entered Okinawa under U.S. military occupation after the war. Its argument is that food was, from the start, a tool of governance.

In the first years after 1945, Okinawans faced famine. Many had been taught the Americans were monsters — and then a U.S. soldier handed over a can of food. One survivor called that first taste “the taste of heaven.” But the relief food came with a condition: people worked on military projects in exchange for rations. Refuse the labor, and the aid could stop. Under the guise of charity, food became coercion. The warm feeling of gratitude and the hard fact of dependence arrived together.

Over the following decades, that history was slowly normalized. Foods born of the bases — luncheon meat (SPAM), taco rice, A&W root beer, Blue Seal ice cream — became beloved local fusion dishes. Today they are sold to visitors as the taste of a charming, exotic island. The thesis calls this a process of depoliticization: the occupation is commodified into a nostalgic souvenir, and the memory of coercion is quietly erased. What looks like a friendly local speciality is, on closer reading, a far cry from a neutral plate of food.

Put the three together and a pattern appears. In each case a powerful outsider appropriates a local food culture and renames it on their own terms — as something to admire, to look down on, or to sell. In each case the people being looked at are pushed into a subordinate role, their own meanings taken for granted or ignored. And in each case the politics is easy to turn a blind eye to, precisely because food feels so warm, so personal, so harmless.

This is where Taiwan Travelogue is at its sharpest. It sets the stage for exactly these questions, and then refuses to answer them for us. Chizuko never quite comes to terms with her own power; the novel leaves that work to the reader. So the next time a dish is described as “authentic,” “exotic,” or “just like home,” it is worth asking the travelogue's real question: whose home, and who is doing the calling?

§Grammar patterns to watch 文法重點

1 · Past participle as a reduced clause　過去分詞作後位修飾
Presented as a memoir, the book is… = (Although it is) presented as a memoir…
句首的過去分詞（Presented / Translated / Written…）等於省略了「which is / although it is」。先找真正的主詞與主要動詞，再把分詞片語當作補充說明。
2 · What-cleft for emphasis　what 名詞子句（強調）
What she is really doing is measuring Taiwan…
「What + 主詞 + 動詞 + is…」用來把焦點放在後面那件事，等於「她真正在做的，其實是…」。寫作時可用來製造強調效果。
3 · not so much X as Y　與其說X不如說Y
The food is not so much a bridge as a mirror.
否定前者、肯定後者：「與其說是橋樑，不如說是一面鏡子。」常用來修正或精準化一個比喻。
4 · Having + p.p.　完成式分詞
Having survived the war, Chi-chan is the one who… = After she had survived…
「Having + 過去分詞」表示比主要動詞「更早發生」的事。讀時自動補上「在…之後」。
5 · far from + -ing　遠非、絕不
Far from being innocent, that praise… = It is not innocent at all…
「Far from + 動名詞/形容詞」是強烈否定：「不但不…，反而…」。看到 far from 先預期一個反轉。
6 · It is here that…　it 強調句
It is here that the real question appears.
「It is + 強調的部分 + that…」把句中某一成分挑出來強調。此處強調「就在這裡 / 正是在這一點上」。

§Questions for discussion 討論題

  1. Chizuko believes she is being open-minded when she praises Taiwanese food. Can admiration and power exist at the same time? Have you ever felt “praised” in a way that was also a little condescending?
  2. Why do you think the novel keeps Chi-chan so silent for most of the book — and then gives her the final word as the “translator”? What is the writer arguing through that structure?
  3. Twain attacks European food and praises American food. Is national pride through cuisine harmless fun, or can it also be a kind of border-building? Think of an example from your own culture.
  4. In Okinawa, the same can of food meant both survival and control. Can a gift be generous and coercive at once? Where do you see “gifts with conditions” today?
  5. The Okinawa thesis says military history was “depoliticized” into a tourist souvenir. Are there foods or places in Taiwan whose difficult history has been turned into something cute or marketable?
  6. After reading all three texts, has your view of the word “authentic” on a menu changed? Who usually decides what counts as authentic?
